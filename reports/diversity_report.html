Which human population is the most “mutation-rich”?

Genetic diversity across 20 worldwide populations · AADR 1240K (diploid SGDP/HGDP) · 2026-06-29
Framing: de-novo mutation rate (~1.2×10⁻⁸/bp/gen) is ~constant across humans and needs trios to measure. We measure the panel-observable proxy — standing genetic diversity (heterozygosity) — and answer "which population carries the most accumulated variation."
The most genetically diverse human population is African. By direct measurement the West African Yoruba ranks first (H=0.2787, #1 on both panels). The deeper, ascertainment-corrected answer is the Khoesan (Ju|'hoan / San): SNP panels ascertained mostly in non-San peoples undercount them — pushed to #17 of 20 on the Eurasian-leaning 1240K panel, they leap to #4 on the balanced Human Origins panel, and on whole-genome data they are the single most diverse people on Earth (Mallick 2016). Lowest on every panel: Native Americans (Karitiana, H=0.1779) — a 1.57× spread. Diversity falls with distance from Africa (r=-0.74, p=1.8e-04).

Ascertainment bias — why the panel matters (and why the San are really #1)

The 1240K SNP set was discovered mostly in non-San populations, so it undercounts variation private to deeply-diverging Africans and deflates their heterozygosity. The same shotgun-diploid individuals scored on the balanced Human Origins panel rise sharply: the Khoesan (Ju|'hoan) move from #17 on 1240K to #4 on Human Origins (of 20 shared populations). The 1240K "winner" reflects ascertainment as much as biology; the balanced panel restores the textbook result that the Khoesan are the most diverse humans.

Why — the reasoning

  1. Africa is the homeland and reservoir of diversity. Modern humans arose in Africa ~300 kya; African populations have had the longest time and largest long-term effective size to accumulate variation.
  2. The Khoesan (Ju|'hoan / San) top the list — among the earliest-diverging human lineages (~200–300 kya) with a large effective size, hence the most independent ancestral lineages and the highest heterozygosity of any living people.
  3. The out-of-Africa bottleneck (~50–70 kya): every non-African descends from a small founder group, discarding much African variation in one step.
  4. Serial founder effects: each onward migration sampled a subset of the last, so heterozygosity falls nearly linearly with distance from Africa (Ramachandran 2005; Figure 2). Native Americans (Karitiana), at the end of the longest migration through repeated bottlenecks, are least diverse; Oceanians and isolates (Papuans, Kalash) are low for their regions.
  5. Demography, not a faster clock: the per-generation mutation rate is essentially identical everywhere, so these are differences in population size and history, not mutational biology. (The mutation spectrum does vary subtly between populations — e.g. the European TCC→TTC pulse, Harris 2015 — but that is about mutation types, not overall rate, and is not resolvable here.)

Caveats

The 1240K panel is ascertained, which affects absolute heterozygosity (the African-highest gradient is robust regardless); some samples are few (Ju|'hoan n≈10, SEs shown); heterozygosity is one of several concordant diversity measures.
